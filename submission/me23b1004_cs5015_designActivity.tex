\section{Violations Identified \& Solutions}

I conducted a literature survey of \textbf{Practo.com} and identified \textbf{14 usability violations}. For each violation, I built a targeted solution in my \textbf{React + Tailwind CSS} prototype (\textit{Better Practo}), applying HCI principles from Lectures 1--8.

\vspace{0.5cm}

% ================================================================
% V1
% ================================================================
\subsection{V1: Dense Footer with 50+ Links}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's footer contains \textbf{50+ links} spread across 6 columns. The ``For Patients'' column alone has 12+ items. This overwhelms users with too many choices.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{practo_footer.png}
    \caption{Practo's footer --- 50+ links across 6 columns}
\end{figure}

\begin{hcibox}{Miller's Law (7$\pm$2), Hick's Law, Tesler's Law, Nielsen \#8}
\textbf{Miller's Law}: humans hold 7$\pm$2 chunks in working memory; 50+ links far exceeds this. \textbf{Hick's Law}: decision time increases with number of choices. \textbf{Tesler's Law}: complexity should be absorbed by the system. \textbf{Nielsen \#8}: aesthetic and minimalist design --- every extra link competes with relevant content.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My footer uses \textbf{4 collapsible sections} with $\leq$7 items each. On mobile, sections are \textbf{accordion-style} (tap to expand). Total visible links reduced from 50+ to a manageable set.
\end{solutionbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{our_footer_b.png}
    \caption{Better Practo --- Clean footer with 4 sections, $\leq$7 items each}
\end{figure}

\subsection{V2: Dual Search Bar (Location + Query)}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's homepage has \textbf{two separate search inputs} side-by-side: one for location and one for query. Users must fill both before searching, adding unnecessary friction.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{practo_homepage.png}
    \caption{Practo's dual search bar --- two separate inputs}
\end{figure}

\begin{hcibox}{Tesler's Law, Zipf's Law (Least Effort), Asimov's 2nd Law}
\textbf{Tesler's Law}: system should absorb complexity --- auto-detecting location is trivial. \textbf{Zipf's Law}: users follow the path of least effort --- two inputs when one suffices adds friction. \textbf{Asimov's 2nd Law}: don't waste user's time.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
I provide a \textbf{single unified search bar} for doctors, specialties, or symptoms. Location is \textbf{auto-detected} and displayed as a changeable chip (``Chennai''). One input, zero friction.
\end{solutionbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{our_home_b.png}
    \caption{Better Practo --- Unified search with auto-detected location chip}
\end{figure}

\subsection{V3: Cluttered Doctor Cards}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's doctor cards show \textbf{everything at once}: name, specialty, experience, location, hospital, fee, rating, patient stories, badges, and two CTAs. This creates information overload.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{practo_search_results.png}
    \caption{Practo's search results --- dense cards with too much information}
\end{figure}

\begin{hcibox}{Inverted Pyramid, Nielsen \#8 (Aesthetic Minimalism), Progressive Disclosure}
\textbf{Inverted Pyramid}: most important information first, details progressively revealed. \textbf{Nielsen \#8}: don't clutter with rarely-needed info. \textbf{Progressive Disclosure}: show essentials initially, reveal details on demand.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My cards show only \textbf{essentials} upfront: name, specialty, rating, availability. Additional details (education, hospital, about) are behind a \textbf{``More details''} expand button --- tighter progressive disclosure.
\end{solutionbox}

\subsection{V4: No Onboarding for First-Time Users}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo provides \textbf{no onboarding experience} for first-time users. New users land directly on the homepage with no guidance on how to use the platform, find doctors, or book appointments. This increases the learning curve and may cause drop-offs.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{hcibox}{Learnability, Familiarity, Power Law of Practice}
\textbf{Learnability}: a key usability principle --- new users should quickly understand how to use the system. \textbf{Familiarity}: onboarding uses icons and language users already recognize. \textbf{Power Law of Practice}: guided onboarding accelerates the user's learning curve, reducing time-to-proficiency.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
I implemented a \textbf{3-step guided onboarding tour} that appears as a modal overlay on first login:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Step 1}: ``Find Trusted Doctors'' --- explains the search feature
    \item \textbf{Step 2}: ``Book Instantly'' --- explains the 3-step booking
    \item \textbf{Step 3}: ``Get Care, Your Way'' --- explains video consult \& prescriptions
\end{itemize}
Features: animated progress dots, gradient illustrations, and a \textbf{``Skip'' button} always visible (respecting User Control / Shneiderman \#7).
\end{solutionbox}

\subsection{V5 + V14: Weak Booking Feedback \& No Progress Indicator}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's booking flow has \textbf{no progress indicator} --- users don't know how many steps remain. The ``Continue'' button is greyed out with no explanation. After confirming, there is no meaningful success feedback. The only escape is ``Go back to my results.''
\end{violationbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{subfigure}[b]{0.48\textwidth}
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{practo_booking_slots.png}
        \caption{Slot selection --- no progress bar}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    \begin{subfigure}[b]{0.48\textwidth}
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{practo_booking.png}
        \caption{Details page --- no step count}
    \end{subfigure}
    \caption{Practo's booking flow --- no progress indicator, weak feedback}
\end{figure}

\begin{hcibox}{Closure (Shneiderman \#4), Synthesizability, Shneiderman \#3 (Feedback), Shneiderman \#5 (Easy Reversal)}
\textbf{Closure}: sequences should have clear beginning, middle, and end. \textbf{Synthesizability}: users should see where they are in a process. \textbf{Shneiderman \#3}: every action needs feedback. \textbf{Shneiderman \#5}: users should be able to reverse actions easily.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My booking has a \textbf{3-step progress bar}: \textit{Select Slot $\rightarrow$ Confirm $\rightarrow$ Booked!}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Progress indicator}: Step numbers (1/3, 2/3, 3/3) with filling bar
    \item \textbf{Reversal}: ``$\leftarrow$ Change Slot'' on Step 2 goes back to Step 1
    \item \textbf{No Reset button}: Individual field clear ($\times$) instead (Reset Must Die)
    \item \textbf{Success animation}: Green checkmark + summary on Step 3 (Closure)
    \item \textbf{Loader}: Spinner with ``Booking...'' during submission
\end{itemize}
\end{solutionbox}

    \centering

    \caption{Better Practo --- 3-step progress bar: Select Slot $\rightarrow$ Confirm $\rightarrow$ Booked!}

Practo's help is on a \textbf{separate subdomain} (\texttt{help.practo.com}), breaking context. The FAQ content is dense and not categorized by user task.

\begin{solutionbox}
My Help is \textbf{built into the app} (same domain, navbar link). FAQs are \textbf{categorized by task} (Booking, Consultations, Payments, Privacy) with a \textbf{search bar}. Accordion layout with concise headings.

\subsection{V7: Small Text in Secondary Areas}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo uses text below 14px in footer links, secondary labels, and card metadata, reducing readability for older or visually impaired users.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{hcibox}{Accessibility, Observability (Robustness)}
\textbf{Accessibility} standards recommend $\geq$16px body text, $\geq$14px secondary. \textbf{Observability}: users must perceive all relevant information --- small text undermines this.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My app enforces \textbf{minimum 14px} font size globally. Body text is 16px. I use the \textbf{Inter} font family for readability. Proper contrast ratios maintained throughout.

\textit{This is a global design fix applied across all pages --- see any page in my prototype for verification.}
\end{solutionbox}

\subsection{V9: Password Fully Masked Without Toggle}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's login masks the password completely with no option to reveal it. Users cannot verify what they typed, leading to errors and frustration.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{practo_password.png}
    \caption{Practo's login --- password fully masked with no reveal option}
\end{figure}

\begin{hcibox}{Stop Password Masking (Lecture 5), Norman's Feedback, Nielsen \#5 (Error Prevention)}
\textbf{Stop Password Masking}: masking causes more harm (typos, frustration) than good (shoulder surfing is rare). \textbf{Norman's Feedback}: users need to see effect of actions. \textbf{Nielsen \#5}: letting users see their password prevents typos.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My login has a \textbf{show/hide password toggle} (eye icon). Default: masked. Click eye to reveal. Balances security with usability.
\end{solutionbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{our_login_b.png}
    \caption{Better Practo --- Show/hide password toggle (password ``password123'' visible)}
\end{figure}

\subsection{V10: Important Content Not Left-Loaded}

\begin{violationbox}
On several Practo pages, important actions are placed on the right (e.g., ``Book Clinic Visit'' CTA, map sidebar), competing with the primary content scanning path.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{hcibox}{Horizontal Attention (69\% Left), F-Pattern, Above the Fold}
Research shows \textbf{69\% of viewing time} is on the left half. The \textbf{F-Pattern} describes how users scan: across top, then down the left. \textbf{Above the Fold}: 80\% of time spent above the fold.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
All pages follow the \textbf{F-Pattern}: primary content left-aligned. Doctor profile: main info takes 2/3 width (left), booking CTA on right 1/3. Search bar and hero cards are centered/left-loaded. Key info always above the fold.

\textit{This is a global layout principle applied across all pages --- see any page in my prototype for verification.}
\end{solutionbox}

\subsection{V11: Reset Buttons on Forms}

\begin{violationbox}
Traditional form patterns include ``Reset'' or ``Clear All'' buttons that can accidentally destroy all user input, causing frustration.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{hcibox}{Reset Must Die (Lecture 6), Shneiderman \#5 (Easy Reversal), Asimov's 1st Law}
\textbf{Reset Must Die}: reset buttons are destructive and rarely desired. \textbf{Shneiderman \#5}: reversal should be granular, not all-or-nothing. \textbf{Asimov's 1st Law}: don't harm the user (data loss = harm).
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My forms have \textbf{no reset button}. Instead: individual \textbf{clear buttons ($\times$)} per field, ``$\leftarrow$ Change Slot'' for granular step reversal, and form data preserved between steps.

\textit{See the booking flow (Figures for V5+V14) for verification --- no reset button anywhere in the form.}
\end{solutionbox}

\subsection{V12: Default Sort is Alphabetical / Unclear}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's search sorting criteria is opaque and inconsistent. Alphabetical ordering reflects system convenience, not the user's mental model.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{hcibox}{Alphabetical Sorting Must Die (Lecture 6), Hick's Law}
\textbf{Alphabetical Must Die}: users think in terms of ``best rated'' or ``cheapest,'' not alphabetical. \textbf{Hick's Law}: clear sort options reduce decision time.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
I offer \textbf{explicit sort pills}: Relevance (default), Rating, Experience, Fee. Active sort is visually highlighted. Default is \textbf{Relevance} (rating $\times$ reviews). No alphabetical option.

\textit{See the Find Doctors page (Figure for V8 navbar) --- sort pills visible at top of results.}
\end{solutionbox}

\subsection{V13: Forced City Selection Modal}

\begin{violationbox}
Practo's Lab Tests page forces a \textbf{city selection modal} before showing any content. Users cannot browse without selecting a city first.
\end{violationbox}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{practo_city_selection.png}
    \caption{Practo forces city selection before showing content}
\end{figure}

\begin{hcibox}{Asimov's 2nd Law (Don't Waste Time), Zipf's Law (Least Effort)}
\textbf{Asimov's 2nd Law}: don't waste user's time --- auto-detect if possible. \textbf{Zipf's Law}: users follow the path of minimal effort.
\end{hcibox}

\begin{solutionbox}
My app \textbf{auto-detects the city} (shown as a chip in the search bar) and lets users change with a single tap. No blocking modals. Content loads immediately.
\end{solutionbox}
